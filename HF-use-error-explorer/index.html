Worked Devices
The distinction students miss:
In a use error, the device performs exactly as designed — the user's action (or inaction) starts the chain to harm. In a component failure, the user does everything right and the hardware/software fails. The second belongs in your FMEA. Both feed the same ISO 14971 risk management file.
Grounded in IEC 62366-1 (usability engineering process) and FDA's 2016 guidance Applying Human Factors and Usability Engineering to Medical Devices. Severity scale modeled on common ISO 14971 practice — ISO 14971 does not prescribe a specific scale.

Risk-Control Hierarchy
In order — ISO 14971 / IEC 62366-1
①
Inherent safety by design
Remove the hazard or make the error impossible / inconsequential
Most effective
②
Protective measures & safeguards
Interlocks, guards, alarms — catch the error before harm
Middle
③
Information for safety
Labels, IFU, training — relies on attention & memory
Least effective
Both ISO 14971 and IEC 62366-1 require risk-control options to be considered in this order. FDA's 2016 HF guidance is blunt: design changes are the most effective way to reduce use-related risk; labeling and training are the least. A labeling-only control for a critical task invites scrutiny.
Use-Related vs Component-Failure Risk
This app vs your FMEA
Use-related (here)Component failure (FMEA)
Starts withUser action or inaction — device works as designedPart, software, or process fails — user did everything right
Found byTask analysis, formative studies, known use-error dataDesign/process FMEA, fault trees, reliability data
ProbabilityRarely quantifiable — prioritize by severity of harmOften estimable from component failure rates
Controlled byUI design, safeguards, then labelingRedundancy, derating, robust design, process control
Same device, two different analyses — and both land in the same ISO 14971 risk management file. When a field event report arrives, deciding which chain it started is exactly the manufacturer's job.
