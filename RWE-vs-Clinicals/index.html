RWE vs Clinical Trials

Interactive Decision Simulator

Real-World Evidence (RWE) is data collected outside a controlled study — patient registries, electronic health records, insurance claims. A prospective clinical trial uses a pre-specified protocol, controlled enrollment, and often randomization to generate evidence under controlled conditions.

What's at stake: FDA approval determines whether a device reaches patients. The evidence standard you choose affects both how quickly that happens and how confidently we know the device is safe and effective. Getting it wrong in either direction has real consequences — delayed access kills patients who needed the device sooner; premature approval harms patients when the device turns out to be worse than expected.

This simulator uses simplified models — real approval decisions involve dozens of additional factors — but the core tradeoffs are drawn from published cases and actual FDA precedent.

📖 What do these risk tiers mean?

These categories come from cardiac surgery risk scoring (the STS score) and are used here as a general proxy for how much a patient needs this device versus having viable alternatives.

Prohibitive Risk
Cannot survive the procedure, or has no surgical/treatment alternative at all. For non-cardiac devices: patients with no equivalent diagnostic option.
High Risk
Eligible for existing treatment, but mortality or morbidity risk is substantial. The device offers a meaningful advantage over the alternative.
Intermediate Risk
Borderline candidate for existing treatment. The benefit-risk balance between the new device and the standard of care is genuinely uncertain.
Low Risk
Healthy enough that existing treatment is routine and low-risk. The new device needs to prove it's at least as good — and preferably better.

📖 Why do real-world rates almost always exceed trial rates?

This gap is not a coincidence — it has three structural causes that every device engineer should understand:

  • Selection bias: Trials enroll healthier patients who meet strict eligibility criteria. Real-world registries include everyone — older patients, sicker patients, and those with comorbidities that trials excluded. The same device performs worse in a broader, sicker population.
  • Operator variation: Trials are performed at high-volume expert centers with protocol-mandated technique. Real-world use spans community hospitals where procedure volumes are lower and outcomes are consistently worse. This is especially true for implantable devices and surgical procedures.
  • Surveillance intensity: Trial patients are followed on a fixed schedule with mandated imaging and lab work. Real-world patients miss follow-ups, switch providers, or have complications attributed to other causes — meaning real-world adverse event rates are often undercounted, not overcounted.

Note on "standard of care": This is not "no treatment" — it's the currently approved and reimbursed treatment. For TAVR it's open-heart surgery (SAVR). For COVID testing it's PCR. For dementia screening it's clinical neuropsychological assessment. A new device has to beat or match that bar, not beat nothing.

RWE Pathway
Fast approval, real-world evidence
Approve immediately using existing registry data. All patients receive the device from day one, without waiting for a prospective study.
What this actually looks like: The manufacturer submits existing registry data — for example, the STS/ACC TVT Registry for cardiac devices — as the primary evidence package. The FDA evaluates data quality, sample size, completeness, and potential confounding. Approval can happen in months rather than years. Post-market surveillance requirements are typically attached as a condition of approval, but enforcement has historically been weak. There is no control arm, no randomization, and no pre-specified primary endpoint in the traditional sense.

Clinical Trial
Slower, controlled evidence
Require a 4-year prospective IDE trial. Patients wait for the device; prohibitive-risk patients, who have no alternative, receive the device immediately.
What this actually looks like: The manufacturer submits an Investigational Device Exemption (IDE) application to the FDA. After approval, the trial requires IRB sign-off at each enrolling site, informed consent from every patient, randomization to treatment vs. control arms, pre-specified primary endpoints (typically all-cause mortality or major adverse events at 2 years), and an independent Data Safety Monitoring Board that can halt the trial early. Enrollment itself takes 1–2 years before follow-up even begins — that's where most of the delay comes from, not the follow-up period.

Hybrid Approach
Targeted fast-track + trial
Immediate access for prohibitive-risk patients who have no alternative. An accelerated 2-year trial for all other risk groups.
What this actually looks like: The trial isn't simply shortened arbitrarily — the FDA accepts a smaller, more focused trial because the highest-need population (prohibitive risk) already satisfies the benefit-risk threshold without further study. The remaining populations enroll in a streamlined IDE trial with a 2-year primary endpoint, often with a single-arm design or a registry-controlled comparison. Broader expansion to low-risk populations waits for full trial data. This mirrors FDA's Breakthrough Device Designation program, which accelerates review for devices serving serious conditions without adequate alternatives.

🧭 When the numeric winner isn't the right answer

The pathway with the lowest adverse event count is not automatically the right choice. Regulators and engineers must also weigh:

Is the harm reversible? An implanted mesh that causes chronic pain cannot be easily removed. A wearable BP monitor that gives inaccurate readings can simply be turned off. Irreversibility raises the bar for how confident you need to be before approving.
Does a proven alternative exist? If patients have a safe, effective alternative, the new device must demonstrate clear superiority — not just equivalence — before RWE alone is sufficient to justify approval.
Can post-market surveillance realistically catch signals? Telemetry-enabled pacemakers generate continuous performance data. Hernia mesh complications emerge as chronic pain over years, in patients who've switched providers and may not connect their pain to the implant. The FDA's ability to "course-correct" after approval depends entirely on this.
Is the benefit-risk ratio above FDA's implicit threshold? FDA doesn't publish a numeric threshold, but reviewers apply a "reasonable assurance of safety and effectiveness" standard. For life-threatening conditions with no alternatives, that bar is lower. For wellness devices with many competitors, it's higher.
Historical anchors:
✓ RWE done right — TAVR (2011–2019)

TAVR was approved for prohibitive-risk patients in 2011 using a single RCT (PARTNER 1B), then expanded incrementally to high, intermediate, and low-risk patients as evidence accumulated. Each expansion used a combination of RCT and registry data. The FDA matched evidence requirements to patient need — the most urgent patients got access first, lower-risk patients waited for stronger evidence. This staged approach is now the model for major structural heart devices.

✗ RWE done wrong — Surgical Mesh (1990s–2010s)

Hernia mesh and pelvic floor mesh devices were cleared through the 510(k) pathway — which required only substantial equivalence to a predicate device, not clinical trial evidence of safety. By the time chronic pain, erosion, and organ perforation data accumulated in registries, hundreds of thousands of patients had already been implanted. The FDA issued safety communications in 2008, 2010, and 2011, and ultimately reclassified pelvic mesh as a Class III device in 2016 — but could not undo the harm already done. This case is the primary reason the FDA tightened mesh requirements and is frequently cited in design control training.
