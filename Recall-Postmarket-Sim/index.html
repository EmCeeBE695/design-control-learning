Regulatory Quick Reference
21 CFR 803 — Medical Device Reporting. Deaths & serious injuries: 30 calendar days. Malfunctions likely to cause/contribute to death or serious injury if they recur: 30 days. Events needing remedial action against an unreasonable risk of substantial public-health harm: 5 work days (803.53).
21 CFR 7 — Recalls (voluntary). You initiate and run a health hazard evaluation (7.41); FDA assigns the class. Class I: reasonable probability of serious adverse health consequences or death. II: temporary/reversible, or remote probability of serious. III: not likely to cause adverse health consequences.
21 CFR 806 — Report corrections & removals made to reduce a risk to health within 10 working days of initiating. Non-reportable ones still get records (806.20).
21 CFR 820.100 — CAPA: analyze quality data, investigate root cause, act to correct and prevent recurrence, verify effectiveness. Complaints flow in via 820.198.
Framework trainer. This simulation compresses months of postmarket work into one sitting. Timelines, point values, and "field harm" estimates are pedagogical devices — not a depiction of how a real quality system, FDA review, or recall actually unfolds. The device, company, and events are fictional.
You are the postmarket surveillance lead.
Your company's cleared glucose meter, VeriGluc Home, has been on the market for 14 months. Complaints arrive over the next 90 simulated days — most are noise, a few are a real safety signal. Triage each report, make the MDR call under 21 CFR 803, decide when to escalate, run the recall decision under 21 CFR 7 & 806, and close the loop with a CAPA under 820.100. Wrong triage lets harm accumulate in the field. Over-reporting burns your team and your credibility. Both are graded.
